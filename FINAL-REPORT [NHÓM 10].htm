1.     Introduction

In the current competitive job market, knowing the determinants of student placement has become important to educational institutions, prospective students, and businesses. The main objectives of this paper are to describe the dataset comprehensively through descriptive statistics, and analytics and to highlight relationships and patterns that represent factors that influence students' salaries and employability. This study applies both descriptive and analytical approaches to tackle basic statistical measures and more intricate associations within the data. Descriptive analytics involves measuring parameters, such as average student salary, distribution of academic grades at the different levels of education (SSC, HSC, degree, MBA), and the proportion of students with prior work experience. Furthermore, the analytics will study relationships more elaborately to determine the influence of factors on salary and job acceptance through regression and classification. Therefore, the ultimate goal is to generate recommendations to guide educators, students, and employers in decision-making. Through this analysis, the research group hopes to contribute valuable knowledge to the field of education analytics and promote better alignment between industry needs and academic expectations.

2.     Data

2.1. Data Sources

For our project, the data is sourced from a dataset provided by Ben Roshan on Kaggle [1].

2.2. Dataset Overview

The dataset our group selected provides detailed information on factors that influence students' academic performance, work experience, and internship outcomes. Each student value is identified by a unique sl_no. Key columns for analysis include 'status and salary' (for placement status and salary), ssc_p, hsc_p, degree_p, mba_p (for academic performance), workex and etest_p (for work experience and employability test) and specialisation (for specialisation). This dataset structure provides valuable information for our group to analyse the impact of different factors on student salaries and employability.

2.3. Data Preparation 


The dataset contains information about 215 students, primarily focused on academic and employment-related attributes. That means, the dataset contains 215 entries with 15 columns, including both numerical columns (such as ssc_p, hsc_p, degree_p, etest_p, mba_p, salary) and categorical columns (such as gender, ssc_b, hsc_b, hsc_s, degree_t, workex, specialisation, status).

Attributes


·       sl_no: Serial number of the student (unique identifier).

·       ssc_p: Secondary Education percentage.

·       hsc_p: Higher Secondary Education percentage.

·       degree_t: Type of degree (e.g., Sci&Tech, Comm&Mgmt).

·       workex: Work experience (Yes/No).

·       etest_p: Employability test percentage.

·       specialisation: MBA specialisation (e.g., Mkt&Fin, Mkt&HR).

·       mba_p: MBA percentage.

·       status: Employment status (Placed/Not Placed).

·       salary: Salary offered (in monetary units, with missing values indicating unplaced students).

Data types of each factor

We have 1 integer, 5 float and 8 object data types in our dataset.

2.3.2. Checking Missing Values

The dataset contains missing values in the salary column, which represents the salary offered to students. The missing values in the salary column are associated with students who are marked as "Not Placed" in the status column. There are 67 null values in our data, which means 67 unhired candidates. Since the missing values ​​are significant (indicating unplaced students), we cannot eliminate these values. We can't impute it with mean/median values and it will go against the context of this dataset and it will show unhired candidates got salary. 

           

2.3.3. Handling Missing Values

If we drop the rows which have null values, we might sabotage some potential information from the dataset. So to address missing values, we impute the values ​​into the NaN records which leads us to accurate models. Since it is a salary feature, the best practice we use is to impute '0' for unhired candidates.

Looking at the graph, it is evident that the highest level of accuracy is seen in Logistic Regression at 83%, followed by KNN (78%) and Random Forest (77%), which indicates that Logistic Regression is the most suitable method in terms of handling the given dataset. The rationale behind the higher level of accuracy seen in Logistic Regression over KNN and Random Forest in the data graph is that the relationship between the features and the target variation is linear. In the case of simple, less noisy, and linearly structured data, Logistic Regression has better generalization ability; whereas; KNN is prone to denormalized data and over-selection of the K parameter and lack of complexity in the data can waste the immense potential of the ensemble model in Random Forest. For the above analysis, Logistic Regression is shown to be the most


suitable method, producing a better level of accuracy than the other two methods in this case. If there is room for improvement, we suggest that our team could experiment with advanced techniques like Random Forest tuning or combining different models.
